Founding Thesis

The Intelligence Layer

Why GEO replaces SEO, token economics reshapes how AI is procured, and structured market intelligence becomes the infrastructure play no one is building yet.

By Daniel Yarmoluk Graphify.md March 2026

There is a structural shift underway in how businesses interact with information — and almost no one is positioned to capture it. Not because the opportunity is obscure, but because it sits between two disciplines that rarely talk to each other: data infrastructure and AI application design.

This is an essay about that gap. About why the way AI answers questions today is broken in a specific, measurable, fixable way. About what fixes it. And about why the fix is a business, not a feature.

The thesis has four parts. They build on each other, but they are each independently true.

Part I

GEO Is the New SEO — and No One Has Built the Infrastructure for It

In 2024, Andreessen Horowitz published an essay arguing that Generative Engine Optimization — getting your company cited in AI answers — would eventually matter more than traditional search rankings. The argument was directionally correct. What the essay didn't address was how.

SEO was always a game of structure. Google didn't rank pages randomly. It ranked pages that were organized in specific ways — with the right tags, the right link architecture, the right relationship between content and authority. Businesses that understood the structure won. Businesses that dumped unstructured content onto the web lost, regardless of quality.

GEO works the same way. LLMs don't cite sources randomly. They cite structured, authoritative, relationship-rich content that appears repeatedly across their training and retrieval context. The companies that will appear inside AI answers in 2027 are the ones building structured entity presence today.

"The companies that will appear inside AI answers in 2027 are the ones building structured entity presence today. Not because of advertising. Because of architecture."

Not because of advertising. Not because of prompt engineering. Because of architecture.

The problem is that no one has built the infrastructure layer that makes GEO executable at scale. There is no tool that takes a company's market position — its relationships to competitors, customers, regulatory bodies, capital sources, and adjacent industries — and structures it into a form that AI systems can reliably retrieve, cite, and reason about.

That infrastructure is a knowledge graph. Compressed to structured markdown. Updated weekly. Distributed to every AI workflow that touches that vertical.

That is what Graphify.md builds.

The GEO Stack

SEO era: structured HTML + backlinks + keyword density = Google ranking

GEO era: structured entities + relationship graphs + authoritative co-occurrence = LLM citation

The game changed. The underlying logic — structure wins — did not.

Part II

Token Economics Is the Business Model That Nobody Is Talking About

The AI infrastructure conversation is dominated by compute. GPU clusters. Model training runs. Inference costs at the hardware level. These are real costs, but they are not the cost that enterprises are actually bleeding on.

The cost that matters — the one that compounds every week, in every AI deployment, in every enterprise — is the token tax.

The Token Tax, Explained

Every LLM call has a context window. Everything you want the model to know about before it answers has to fit inside that window — and you pay per token. The most common enterprise AI implementation today works like this: take a PDF, shred it into chunks, retrieve the most relevant chunks at query time, stuff them into the context window, and call the model.

This works. It is also extraordinarily wasteful. A typical SEC Form D filing is 8-12 pages of legal boilerplate with 11 fields of actual signal. A GDELT news article is 600 words of narrative with 4-6 entities that matter. A USPTO patent application is 40 pages of legal language with one core claim and a handful of assignee relationships that tell you anything useful about market dynamics.

When you feed these documents raw into an LLM, you pay for all of it. The boilerplate. The legal disclaimers. The redundant language. The formatting artifacts. All of it burns tokens. And you pay for every token, every call, every day.

A Compressed Knowledge Graph file for a single SEC EDGAR Form D filing is 200-400 tokens. The raw filing is 3,000-6,000 tokens. Same information density for the purpose of AI reasoning. One-tenth the cost.

Multiply that across a vertical. A life sciences operator running 500 queries per day against a corpus of recent FDA clearances, EDGAR filings, and patent assignments is spending 10x more than they need to on every single call. Not because of bad engineering. Because the data was never structured for LLM consumption in the first place.

Why No One Has Solved This at the Data Level

The obvious fix — pre-process the documents, extract the signal, structure the relationships — sounds simple. It is not. It requires subject-matter expertise per vertical. A life sciences graph looks nothing like a defense contracting graph. The entities differ. The relationship types differ. The regulatory bodies, funding mechanisms, and key signals differ. A general-purpose ETL pipeline doesn't know that a Phase III trial result changes the valuation landscape for the acquiring entity two nodes away in the graph.

That expertise, embedded into a reusable, updateable, LLM-native format, is the product. Not the pipeline. The intelligence inside the pipeline.

"The value is not the data. The data is free. The value is the structure — the curation layer that decides what matters, what connects to what, and how to represent it in a form that makes every LLM call downstream cheaper and smarter."

Part III

Why Knowledge Graphs Beat RAG — and Why This Isn't a Technical Debate

The retrieval-augmented generation conversation has been almost entirely captured by vector search vendors. The framing is: embed your documents, store the embeddings, retrieve the most similar chunks at query time. This is presented as the state of the art. It is not.

Vector RAG is a lookup. A knowledge graph is a map.

Standard RAG

You query your documents. You retrieve the 10 most similar text chunks. You get a single-hop, linear lookup. The LLM sees flat, unstructured context and produces an answer that is statistically likely — not necessarily accurate to your domain. No reasoning paths. No relationship traversal. No audit trail.

Knowledge Graph

You traverse a structured domain graph. You move through 3-5 relationship hops. At 5 hops across a graph with 200 entities, you have access to ~17,000 reasoning paths — 170x more than vector retrieval across the same corpus. The LLM reasons with full structural context. Every output traces back node by node.

This isn't theoretical. HopRAG (ACL 2025) demonstrated 77% higher answer accuracy over standard RAG using multi-hop graph traversal. Microsoft's 2024 GraphRAG research showed an 83% win rate against vector-only retrieval on complex reasoning tasks. The academic consensus is resolving quickly.

But here is the point that gets lost in the technical discussion: the business case for knowledge graphs doesn't require you to care about reasoning accuracy at all. The token economics alone close the sale. Better reasoning is the margin.

The Format Nobody Designed For LLMs

There is a second problem that compounds the first: almost no data in the world was formatted with LLM consumption in mind. Regulatory filings were designed for human readers and legal archival systems. News articles were designed for human attention. Patent applications were designed for legal defensibility. None of them were designed to minimize token consumption while maximizing semantic density for a language model.

Markdown was. A structured .md file — entities named explicitly, relationships declared between them, attributes attached to nodes, context compressed to the minimum required for reasoning — is natively LLM-readable in a way that no PDF, no HTML document, and no database export is. The model doesn't have to infer structure. The structure is the file.

This is the format Graphify.md builds. Not because it is clever. Because it is the logical conclusion of designing data for the actual consumer: a language model that charges by the token.

Part IV

Market Intelligence by Vertical: The Zero-Cost Data Business That Compounds

The most counterintuitive part of Graphify.md's model is the data cost: zero.

Every piece of market intelligence in our Compressed Knowledge Graph files comes from public sources. The United States government, in its infinite bureaucratic wisdom, has created a near-complete map of the economy and published it for free.

The Five Sources

SEC EDGAR Form D filings are filed within 15 days of every private capital raise in the United States. They contain: company name, industry classification, state of formation, total offering amount, amount sold, number of investors, and sometimes lead investor names. Every venture deal. Every private placement. Every fund raise. Public. Free. Updated daily.

GDELT — the Global Database of Events, Language, and Tone — indexes every major news source in the world in near real-time, classifying events, actors, and sentiment at the article level. Coverage across 65 languages. Updated every 15 minutes. Free.

USPTO patent assignments and applications are public the moment they are filed. A patent assignment tells you who acquired what IP, from whom, and when. It is a direct map of corporate technology acquisition that predates any press release by months.

USASpending.gov contains every federal contract and grant above a threshold, with contractor name, award amount, agency, and period of performance. Defense contracts, NIH grants, DoE awards — all public, all structured, all downloadable.

openFDA contains every drug approval, device clearance, adverse event report, and recall notice the FDA has issued. For life sciences operators, this is the complete regulatory map of the market.

On one side: AI operators. Data science teams, enterprise AI builders, research operations, and LLM application developers who are currently burning tokens on raw documents. They pay for the subscription because the token savings alone justify the cost before intelligence value is even considered. A team running 500 queries per day against life sciences data cuts their monthly token bill by tens of thousands of dollars. The CKG subscription costs a fraction of that.

Conclusion

The Infrastructure Play

Every major platform shift has an infrastructure play that precedes it. The internet had hosting and DNS. Mobile had app distribution and push notification infrastructure. Cloud had identity and API management. Each shift created a layer that every application built on top of needed, whether or not it knew it needed it.

The AI platform shift has a missing layer. Not models — those are commoditizing. Not applications — those are proliferating. The missing layer is structured context: the pre-organized, domain-curated, LLM-native intelligence that sits between raw public data and every AI workflow that needs to reason about markets, companies, and competitive dynamics.

Every AI application built on market intelligence will eventually need this layer. Every enterprise AI team that touches regulatory data, capital markets data, or federal contracting data is paying a token tax today that this layer eliminates. Every company that wants to be cited in AI answers needs structured entity presence that this layer provides.

The moat is not proprietary data. The data is public. The moat is curation depth, vertical coverage, update cadence, and the format standard that makes it all LLM-native. These compound over time. The deeper the vertical coverage, the harder it is to replicate. The more verticals covered, the more complete the market map. The more frequently updated, the more current the intelligence. And the more AI workflows run on top of the files, the more entrenched the format becomes.

This is Graphify.md. Not a data vendor. Not an AI application. The context layer that makes every AI application that needs to reason about markets cheaper, more accurate, and more present in the answers that matter.
