Chart Anatomy — What You're Looking At

Grey Line — Composite Cycle Forecast

The "overall forecast." This is the sum of multiple individual cycles overlaid into a single waveform. It predicts the general direction and magnitude of price movement over the full year.

Built by: identifying dominant cycles in historical price data, projecting them forward, and summing their waveforms into one composite. This is Fourier-style decomposition applied to markets.

Blue Highlight — Strongest Wave (Cycle Sweet Spot)

The section of the composite where the dominant cycle has the highest historical hit rate. On the IBM chart: +80% — meaning price rallied in that direction 80% of the time when this cycle phase appeared historically.

This is the money zone. Williams doesn't trade the whole composite. He isolates the phases where multiple cycles align AND historical accuracy is highest. That's the edge.

Red Line — Short-Term Cycle Swings

Higher-frequency cycle that marks interim highs and lows. "Think of them as reversal areas." This is the timing tool — it tells you WHEN within the broader forecast to enter and exit.

Shorter wavelength cycle, probably 20-40 trading days. Peaks = likely short-term highs (sell/short). Troughs = likely short-term lows (buy/cover). Used for precision entry within the composite direction.

Blue Circles — Projected Cycle Bottoms

Key inflection points where the composite forecasts major lows. These are the highest-conviction long entries in the Williams system.

Where multiple cycle troughs converge. Williams likely marks these when 2+ cycles bottom simultaneously — "cycle cluster lows." These are where he's most aggressive.

Candlesticks — Actual Price (IBM)

Real IBM price action overlaid on the forecast. The proof: price generally follows the composite direction, especially during the blue "sweet spot" zones.

Note the forecast was generated BEFORE these prices occurred. The chart is evidence, not a backtest. Williams publishes these forecasts in advance and tracks them publicly.

Williams' Process — The 6-Layer Stack

Reverse engineered from the chart structure, his published methodology, and 60 years of documented trades.
Layer 1
Historical Data
20-50+ years of daily price data for the target instrument. More data = more reliable cycle detection. Williams uses data going back to the 1960s for major stocks and commodities.
Layer 2
Cycle Extraction
Identify the dominant recurring cycles in the data. Fourier analysis or spectral decomposition to find wavelengths with statistically significant recurrence. Typically finds 3-7 dominant cycles per instrument.
Layer 3
Composite Construction
Sum the individual cycle waveforms into a single composite forecast (the grey line). Each cycle is weighted by its historical reliability and amplitude. The composite IS the forecast.
Layer 4
Sweet Spot Filtering
Identify zones where multiple cycles align AND the historical hit rate exceeds a threshold (80%+ in this chart). These become the blue highlights — the only zones worth trading with full conviction.
Layer 5
Entry Timing
Overlay the short-term cycle (red line) for precision entry. Wait for the red line to trough (buy) or peak (sell) WITHIN a sweet spot zone. This is how Williams avoids entering too early or late.
Layer 6
Trade Execution
Enter at red-line troughs during blue sweet spots. Exit at red-line peaks or when the composite direction changes. Position sizing based on sweet spot conviction level (80%+ = full size).

How to Replicate This — Step by Step

Step 1 — Data

Collect Long-Term Daily Price Data

Minimum 20 years. Williams uses 40-60+ years where available. Daily OHLC (open, high, low, close). The more data, the more cycle periods you can validate.

  • Source: Yahoo Finance, Norgate Data, CSI Data, or Bloomberg
  • Need: daily frequency, adjusted for splits/dividends
  • Critical: clean data with no gaps — cycle detection is sensitive to missing bars
Step 2 — Decompose

Run Spectral / Fourier Analysis

Decompose the price series into constituent cycle wavelengths. Identify the 3-7 dominant cycles by amplitude and statistical significance.

  • Tools: Python (scipy.signal.periodogram), MATLAB, or John Ehlers' MESA (Maximum Entropy Spectral Analysis)
  • Ehlers' MESA is what Williams himself likely uses — specifically designed for market cycle detection
  • Look for cycles between 10 days and 4 years — below 10 days is noise, above 4 years is too infrequent to validate
  • Each cycle should appear in at least 10+ full repetitions to be statistically meaningful
Step 3 — Validate

Test Each Cycle's Predictive Power

For each identified cycle: project it forward into known data and measure how often the directional forecast was correct.

  • Walk-forward validation: train on first 70% of data, test on last 30%
  • Measure: % of time the cycle correctly predicted direction (up/down) at each phase
  • Keep only cycles with 65%+ directional accuracy — Williams' threshold appears to be around 70-80%
  • Track amplitude accuracy too — does the cycle correctly predict MAGNITUDE of moves?
Step 4 — Composite

Build the Composite Forecast

Sum validated cycle waveforms into a single composite. Weight each cycle by its validation accuracy and amplitude.

  • Normalize each cycle to the same scale before summing
  • Weight by accuracy: an 80% cycle gets more weight than a 65% cycle
  • The composite grey line shows the NET predicted direction at each point in time
  • Project forward 12 months — this becomes the annual forecast
Step 5 — Sweet Spots

Identify the High-Conviction Zones

Find the windows where multiple cycles align in the same direction AND the historical hit rate is 80%+. These become the blue highlighted zones.

  • Mark every point where 3+ cycles agree on direction simultaneously
  • Calculate historical accuracy at each convergence zone
  • 80%+ = sweet spot (trade with full conviction)
  • 65-80% = moderate zone (trade with reduced size)
  • Below 65% = stay flat — cycles are conflicting
Step 6 — Execute

Time Entries with the Short-Term Cycle

Within sweet spots, use the red line (short-term cycle) for precision entry. Buy at red-line troughs when composite points up. Sell at red-line peaks when composite points down.

  • Don't enter the moment the sweet spot begins — wait for the red line confirmation
  • Stop loss: below the most recent red-line trough (for longs)
  • Target: next red-line peak or end of sweet spot zone, whichever comes first
  • Position sizing: full size in 80%+ zones, half size in 65-80% zones

What the Chart Reveals About Williams' Specific Method

What cycle lengths is he using?

From the grey line's shape on the IBM chart, the dominant cycle appears to be approximately 180-200 trading days (~9 months). The secondary cycle driving the red line appears to be 20-30 trading days (~1 month). There's likely a 40-60 day intermediate cycle embedded in the composite as well.

Evidence: count the peaks/troughs of each line on the chart. Grey has ~1.5 full cycles per year. Red has ~10-12 oscillations per year.
Why 80% and not 90%?

80% is the sweet spot between conviction and frequency. A 90% threshold exists but produces so few tradeable windows that you'd only get 1-2 trades per year per instrument. 80% gives 3-5 high-conviction trades per year — enough to compound.

Evidence: Williams has said he aims for "3-5 great trades per year" — consistent with an 80% filter producing that frequency.
How does he handle cycle failures?

The chart shows it: look at the May-July 2025 period where actual price diverged from the composite. Williams uses hard stops at the most recent red-line extreme. When a sweet spot fails, the damage is contained to one oscillation of the short-term cycle.

Evidence: Williams' published risk management rule — never risk more than 3% of capital on a single cycle trade. The red line provides the stop level.
Is this Fourier analysis or something else?

Almost certainly Ehlers' MESA (Maximum Entropy Spectral Analysis) or a proprietary variant. Standard Fourier assumes stationary cycles — markets aren't stationary. MESA adapts to cycles that shift in length over time, which is why Williams' forecasts stay accurate as markets evolve.

Evidence: Williams has referenced Ehlers' work. The smoothness of the composite line is characteristic of MESA output, not raw Fourier.
Can this be automated?

The cycle extraction and composite construction — yes, fully automatable. The sweet spot identification — yes. The actual trade entry — partially. Williams still uses discretion for final entry, confirming with volume and market structure. The 80% zone tells him WHEN to look. His experience tells him WHAT to do.

Evidence: Williams has said the forecast is "the map" but the trader still has to "drive the car." He uses additional confirmation (COT data, sentiment, volume) for final execution.
What about the blue circles at the bottom?

These mark projected cycle cluster lows — points where 2+ major cycles bottom simultaneously. These are the highest-conviction long entries in the entire system. Williams likely goes full size on these and holds through the next composite peak.

Evidence: on the chart, actual IBM price bottomed within 1-2 weeks of each blue circle. That's the validation — the projected cluster lows caught real bottoms.
