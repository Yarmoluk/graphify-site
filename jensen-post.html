Contrarian Take

How Jensen Is Wrong
and Andreessen Is Right

Token costs are dropping. Jensen says use more of them. Andreessen says structure wins AI answers. One of them is building toward something real. Here's why the difference is the most important infrastructure bet of the decade.

Daniel Yarmoluk Graphify.md March 2026

Two theses are competing for how AI gets built at the infrastructure level. Jensen Huang's is dominant: tokens are getting cheap, use more of them, bigger context windows, more inference, more compute. Andreessen Horowitz's is quieter but sharper: GEO replaces SEO, structure wins in AI answers, and the companies that build structured entity presence today will own the AI distribution layer tomorrow.

Jensen is right about one thing: token costs have dropped roughly 100x in two years. Inference is commoditizing. On the cost trajectory, he is not wrong.

He is wrong about what that means. And Andreessen — almost accidentally, in a short essay about search — identified the thing that actually matters.

The Fidelity Problem Isn't a Cost Problem

The prevailing assumption in the "tokens are getting cheap" camp is that the main blocker to good AI outputs is context window size. If you could just fit more data in, the model would figure it out. More context, better answers. And as tokens approach free, the logical conclusion is: put everything in. Every document. Every file. Every record. Let the model sort it out.

This is wrong in a way that matters enormously.

The issue is not how much context you can afford to stuff in. The issue is the signal-to-noise ratio of the context you provide. A 200,000-token context window filled with raw PDFs, legal boilerplate, and unstructured text is not 200,000 tokens of intelligence. It is approximately 18,000 tokens of signal buried under 182,000 tokens of noise — and you are asking a language model to find the signal by statistical inference.

"Cheap tokens don't fix bad data. They just make it cheaper to be wrong at scale."

Cheap tokens don't fix bad data. They just make it cheaper to be wrong at scale.

Fidelity — the accuracy, groundedness, and reliability of AI reasoning — is a structural problem, not a cost problem. You cannot buy your way to fidelity with more tokens. You can only achieve it by feeding the model context that is structured for reasoning rather than formatted for human readers.

What Jensen Is Actually Optimizing For

To be fair to Jensen: he is not wrong from Nvidia's perspective. If the response to cheaper tokens is to use more of them — more inference, more compute, more GPU time — that is a great outcome for a company that sells the GPUs. The "abundance mindset" for tokens is a growth thesis for Nvidia's hardware business.

It is not a thesis for AI quality. Those are different things.

The Two Theses

Jensen: Tokens approach zero cost → use more of them → more inference → more compute sold. The constraint is budget. Remove the constraint, flood the context.

Andreessen: AI answers are the new search results → structure determines who gets cited → the companies that build structured entity presence now own AI distribution later. The constraint is architecture. Structure is the moat.

Reality: More raw tokens in context = more noise = lower reasoning fidelity = AI that is confidently wrong, at scale, cheaply. Andreessen describes the solution even while Jensen accelerates the problem.

Enterprise AI teams are not suffering from a token budget problem. They are suffering from a fidelity problem. Their models hallucinate. Their RAG pipelines retrieve the wrong chunks. Their AI answers are plausible but not reliable enough to act on. More tokens will not fix any of this. They will accelerate it.

The Economics Nobody Mentions

Here is what the "tokens are getting cheap" narrative misses: the cost of AI is not just the inference cost. It is the downstream cost of acting on unreliable AI output.

When an analyst asks an AI to summarize the competitive landscape in a life sciences vertical and the AI hallucinates a company's pipeline status, the cost is not the tokens used for that query. The cost is the analyst's time verifying the output, the decision made on incomplete information, the meeting where bad intelligence was presented with confidence.

These costs don't appear in the OpenAI billing dashboard. But they are real, they are large, and they scale with usage — which is exactly what Jensen is encouraging.

Cheap, high-volume, low-fidelity AI is not the answer. It is a more efficient way to be wrong.

What Fidelity Actually Requires

Fidelity in AI reasoning comes from structured context. Not more context. Structured context.

The difference is significant. A structured knowledge graph for a life sciences vertical doesn't tell the model everything. It tells the model the right things, in the right relationships, with the right entity types named explicitly. A Form D filing that has been compressed into a structured .md file — company name, funding stage, lead investor, raise amount, date, therapeutic area, linked to adjacent filings and patent assignments — gives the model a reasoning scaffold. It can traverse relationships. It can make multi-hop inferences. It can verify claims against structure rather than guess from statistical patterns.

Jensen's World

Feed the raw 40-page FDA filing into a 200K context window. Let the model extract what it needs. Tokens are cheap. It'll work itself out. The answer will be statistically plausible.

Structured Context

Feed a 400-token Compressed Knowledge Graph file with the 9 entities and 14 relationships that matter. The model traverses structure. The answer is grounded, verifiable, and traceable back to source — in one-tenth the tokens.

The structured file uses one-tenth the tokens. It produces dramatically more reliable output. And it gets better — not worse — as token costs drop, because the value proposition shifts entirely to quality: the same cost now, dramatically higher fidelity.

In a world of cheap tokens, the competitive advantage is not burning more of them. It is being the team that uses fewer tokens to produce better answers. That is a data architecture problem. And it has a solution.

The Abundance Era Rewards Structure, Not Volume

Here is the paradox of Jensen's world: as tokens get cheap, the constraint on AI quality shifts from cost to signal quality. The teams that win in abundant-compute environments are not the ones dumping the most raw data into the context window. They are the ones who have structured their intelligence most effectively — because that structure is now the scarce resource, not the compute.

Think of it like bandwidth. When internet bandwidth was expensive, the scarce resource was bytes — you optimized ruthlessly. When bandwidth became abundant, the scarce resource became attention — you optimized for what actually reached and moved people. The abundance of one resource elevated the importance of another.

Cheap tokens elevate the importance of structured context. Not because structure becomes more necessary per token — it always was — but because in a world where cost is no longer the filter, fidelity becomes the only differentiator that matters.

"The abundance era doesn't reward teams that use more tokens. It rewards teams whose tokens contain more intelligence per unit. Structure is the moat. Curation is the compound."

This is exactly what Andreessen identified when writing about GEO. He wasn't writing about AI quality per se — he was writing about distribution. But the insight is the same: structured presence wins in the AI layer, just as it won in search. Not because of advertising. Not because of compute. Because of architecture.

Jensen sells the pipes. Andreessen identified what needs to flow through them. The team that structures the intelligence — the actual content of the context, the entity relationships, the curated signal pulled from public data and compressed into LLM-native format — wins regardless of what token prices do.

When tokens are expensive, structure saves you money. When tokens are cheap, structure produces the fidelity that volume cannot. In both markets, structure wins. The only question is whether you build it now or scramble to catch up when it becomes table stakes.

So What Should You Actually Do?

Stop optimizing for token budget. Start optimizing for token fidelity.

The question is not "how many tokens can I afford to use?" The question is "what is the intelligence density of the tokens I am using?" A 400-token structured knowledge graph node that accurately represents an entity, its relationships, and its market position is worth more to an AI reasoning system than 40,000 tokens of raw document that contains the same information buried in legal language.

The practical implication: before you add more data to your AI pipeline, structure the data you have. Extract entities. Name relationships explicitly. Compress to the minimum representation that preserves reasoning fidelity. Build for structure first, scale second.

Jensen will sell you the compute to do either. The question is what you are running on top of it.

Build the intelligence layer first. The tokens can wait.
